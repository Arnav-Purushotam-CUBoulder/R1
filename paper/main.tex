\begin{abstract}
Neural baselines are often recommended for tabular learning, but many applied projects operate under much smaller tuning budgets than contemporary benchmark papers assume. This paper presents a symmetric-budget benchmark of five standard model families on numerical classification tasks drawn deterministically from the \BenchmarkSuiteName{} suite on OpenML. Starting from the full suite, the study retains only active tasks with \MinInstances{} to \MaxInstances{} samples, \MinFeatures{} to \MaxFeatures{} predictor features, \MinClasses{} to \MaxClasses{} classes, no missing values, and no non-numeric predictors after the task target is removed, yielding \DatasetCount{} tasks. Every model receives exactly \SearchConfigsPerModel{} fixed hyperparameter configurations on each of \SeedCount{} random seeds under the same 60/20/20 train/validation/test protocol; no model uses internal early stopping or an additional hold-out split. Across \TotalRuns{} test evaluations and \TotalModelFits{} total fits on a \HardwareCPU{} laptop with \HardwareRAM{} of unified memory, the MLP attains the best mean macro-F1 (\BestMacroFOneValue{}), while HistGradientBoosting attains the best mean rank (\BestMeanRank{}) and best mean expected calibration error (\BestECEValue{}). No model wins a majority of tasks; the MLP wins \MLPTaskWins{}, Extra Trees \ExtraTreesTaskWins{}, HistGradientBoosting \HGBTaskWins{}, Logistic Regression \LogisticTaskWins{}, and Random Forest \RandomForestTaskWins{}. The Friedman test on per-dataset ranks is not significant ($p=\FriedmanPValue$). Holm-corrected post-hoc comparisons against the MLP find no significant differences for HistGradientBoosting, Extra Trees, or Random Forest, while Logistic Regression is significantly worse. Under this symmetric laptop-scale budget, the main result is not a single best default, but a tightly clustered group of non-linear baselines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\PARstart{T}{abular} classification remains one of the most common workloads in applied machine learning, but model-selection advice is often borrowed from papers that assume broad hyperparameter searches, large compute budgets, and benchmark suites assembled for long-running comparative studies. Many practical projects start elsewhere: a single laptop, modest datasets, and enough time for only a small but defensible tuning sweep. In that setting, the real question is not whether neural methods can ever succeed on tabular data, but which baseline families remain competitive when the tuning budget is symmetric and tightly constrained.

Recent literature has made the broader tabular debate more nuanced. Gorishniy \emph{et al.}\ \cite{gorishniy2021} showed that well-regularized MLP baselines are harder to beat than some earlier work implied. Grinsztajn \emph{et al.}\ \cite{grinsztajn2022} then argued that tree-based methods still dominate many typical tabular problems, even under more extensive tuning effort. Those papers are important, but their scope is broader than the smaller-budget decision faced by many practitioners. A laptop-scale benchmark asks a different question: if several standard model families receive the same small validation budget, does any one of them clearly emerge as the default first model?

This paper studies that narrower question on a deterministic subset of numerical tasks from \BenchmarkSuiteName{}. The contribution is not a new learning algorithm. It is a controlled benchmark design that removes two common sources of ambiguity in small empirical studies: ad hoc dataset selection and hidden validation asymmetry across models. The benchmark fixes a transparent inclusion rule, uses the same explicit train/validation/test split for every model, disables estimator-level early stopping that would otherwise consume extra validation data, and evaluates calibration and stability in addition to predictive performance.

The contributions are threefold:
\begin{itemize}
\item A deterministic benchmark construction rule over \BenchmarkSuiteName{} that avoids post hoc dataset curation and yields \DatasetCount{} numerical classification tasks with published OpenML task and dataset IDs.
\item A symmetric protocol in which each model receives exactly \SearchConfigsPerModel{} configurations per seed under the same 60/20/20 split, with no estimator receiving extra hidden validation data.
\item An empirical result that is more restrained than a simple win/loss narrative: under this budget, the strongest non-linear baselines are tightly clustered, the MLP attains the best mean macro-F1, HistGradientBoosting attains the best mean rank and calibration, and no single model wins a majority of tasks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
Open benchmarking infrastructures such as OpenML make it practical to package datasets, code, and evaluation settings in a form that other researchers can inspect and extend \cite{vanschoren2014}. That infrastructure has been especially useful in tabular learning, where conclusions can depend strongly on the exact benchmark composition and protocol.

Classical ensemble methods remain strong baselines for tabular data. Random Forests \cite{breiman2001} and Extremely Randomized Trees \cite{geurts2006} are robust default choices because they tolerate unscaled inputs, train quickly, and often perform well with limited tuning. Gradient boosting methods based on Friedman's additive modeling formulation \cite{friedman2001} are also standard high-performance baselines.

For neural models, two observations from recent work matter most here. First, strong MLP baselines are more competitive than weakly tuned neural baselines suggest \cite{gorishniy2021}. Second, even after broader random-search budgets, tree-based methods often remain stronger on many medium-scale tabular problems \cite{grinsztajn2022}. The present study is narrower than that literature. It does not compare transformers or specialized tabular neural architectures. It asks whether a modest MLP baseline remains competitive when the budget is explicitly small and shared symmetrically across model families.

Because benchmark claims are only convincing when the statistical framing is explicit, the analysis follows Dem\v{s}ar's recommendations for comparing multiple classifiers over multiple datasets \cite{demsar2006}. Calibration is reported with the Brier score \cite{brier1950} and expected calibration error (ECE), following the modern calibration framing popularized by Guo \emph{et al.}\ \cite{guo2017}.

\section{Benchmark Construction}
\subsection{Research Question}
The study tests the following hypothesis: under a fixed laptop-scale search budget on small-to-medium numerical tabular classification tasks, several standard non-linear baselines remain competitive, and any claim of a clear universal default should be supported by a symmetric protocol and task-level statistical evidence.

\subsection{Dataset Inclusion Rule}
The benchmark starts from the full \BenchmarkSuiteName{} classification suite on OpenML. Tasks were retained only if they satisfied all of the following conditions before any experiments were run:
\begin{itemize}
\item active OpenML status;
\item \MinClasses{} to \MaxClasses{} classes;
\item \MinInstances{} to \MaxInstances{} total samples;
\item \MinFeatures{} to \MaxFeatures{} predictor features after excluding the target column;
\item zero missing values according to OpenML metadata;
\item at most one symbolic feature in the raw metadata, to allow for a symbolic target column; and
\item exclusively numeric predictor columns after loading the task-defined target.
\end{itemize}
No manual additions or removals were made after this rule was fixed. Table~\ref{tab:datasets} lists the resulting tasks together with their OpenML task and dataset IDs.

\subsection{Model Families and Search Budget}
Five model families were evaluated:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Logistic Regression with median imputation and standardization.
\item Random Forest with median imputation.
\item Extra Trees with median imputation.
\item HistGradientBoosting with median imputation.
\item A width-limited MLP with ReLU activations, Adam optimization, median imputation, and standardization.
\end{enumerate}

Every dataset-model-seed combination received exactly \SearchConfigsPerModel{} hyperparameter configurations drawn from a fixed model-specific grid. The grids are listed in Appendix~A. The key design choice is budget symmetry: every model is tuned on the same explicit validation split, and no model uses internal early stopping or any other additional validation fraction during fitting. This yields \TotalRuns{} final test evaluations and \TotalModelFits{} total fits when search-stage fits are included.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
For each task and random seed, the data were split with stratified sampling into 60\% train, 20\% validation, and 20\% test partitions. Hyperparameters were selected by validation macro-F1. The chosen configuration was then retrained on the union of the train and validation partitions and evaluated once on the held-out test partition.

The primary ranking metric is macro-F1 because several tasks are imbalanced or multiclass. Accuracy, Brier score, ECE, search time, fit time, and inference time were also recorded. To avoid pseudoreplication, statistical tests were performed on per-dataset mean macro-F1 aggregated over the \SeedCount{} seeds, so each dataset contributed one paired observation per model. The omnibus comparison used the Friedman test \cite{demsar2006}; post-hoc comparisons against the MLP used paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Holm correction.

\subsection{Hardware}
All experiments ran on a single \HardwareCPU{} laptop with \HardwareRAM{} of unified memory running macOS 26.3.1. Summing validation-search and final-fit wall-clock time over all runs yields \BenchmarkWallMinutes{} minutes of total compute. Runtime comparisons should still be interpreted cautiously because they depend on the specific software implementations and estimator-level parallelism available on this hardware.

\section{Discussion}
The most important outcome of this revision is methodological as well as empirical. Once dataset selection is deterministic and hidden validation asymmetries are removed, the benchmark no longer supports a simple narrative in which one top non-linear baseline obviously defeats the others. Instead, the result is a tightly clustered group: the MLP leads in mean macro-F1, HistGradientBoosting leads in mean rank and calibration, Extra Trees offers a strong runtime profile, and Logistic Regression remains a useful speed baseline that still wins a few tasks.

That finding is practically useful. Under a small laptop-scale budget, the right lesson is not to elevate a single universal default, but to begin with a compact and diverse baseline set. On this benchmark, a pragmatic first pass would include one fast linear model, one or two tree ensembles, and a modest MLP rather than betting on only one family. The close clustering among the stronger non-linear models also suggests that benchmark conclusions in this regime are sensitive to protocol design, which makes explicit symmetry and transparent task construction especially important.

This is still a narrow claim. The study does not test transformers, foundation-style pretraining for tabular data, broader searches, or raw high-cardinality categorical feature pipelines. It reports what happens when commonly available model families are tuned under the same small budget on numerical tasks of this scale.

\section{Limitations}
This study has several limitations. Only numerical tasks were included, so the result does not transfer directly to problems dominated by high-cardinality categorical variables or text-derived features. The neural comparison is deliberately modest: it is a small MLP baseline rather than a transformer or a newer specialized tabular architecture. The tuning budget is intentionally small, which suits the target use case but does not test whether broader searches would separate the model families more clearly. Finally, the runtime measurements are implementation-specific and should not be interpreted as framework-independent statements about the intrinsic computational efficiency of each model family.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented a symmetric-budget benchmark of classical baselines and a modest MLP on deterministically selected numerical tasks from \BenchmarkSuiteName{}. With exactly \SearchConfigsPerModel{} configurations per model and the same explicit 60/20/20 split for every estimator, the benchmark does not reveal a single clear winner among the strongest non-linear baselines. The MLP achieves the best mean macro-F1, HistGradientBoosting achieves the best mean rank and calibration, and the Friedman test does not support a significant omnibus separation. For this narrow but common laptop-scale regime, the evidence supports using a small set of complementary baselines rather than assuming one universal default.

\section{Exact Hyperparameter Grids}
Each dataset-model-seed combination evaluated exactly \SearchConfigsPerModel{} hyperparameter configurations before the final refit on train$+$validation data:
\begin{itemize}
\item Logistic Regression: $C \in \{0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100, 1000\}$ and class weight in \{none, balanced\}.
\item Random Forest: 300 trees, max depth in \{none, 12, 24\}, minimum samples per leaf in \{1, 2\}, and max features in \{sqrt, 0.5\}.
\item Extra Trees: 300 trees, max depth in \{none, 12, 24\}, minimum samples per leaf in \{1, 2\}, and max features in \{sqrt, 0.5\}.
\item HistGradientBoosting: learning rate in \{0.03, 0.1\}, max depth in \{none, 6, 12\}, max leaf nodes in \{15, 31\}, minimum samples per leaf fixed at 20, and internal early stopping disabled.
\item MLP: hidden layers in \{(64), (128), (128, 64)\}, $L_2$ penalty in \{$10^{-5}$, $10^{-4}$\}, learning rate in \{$3 \times 10^{-4}$, $10^{-3}$\}, batch size 128, maximum iterations 500, and internal early stopping disabled.
\end{itemize}
